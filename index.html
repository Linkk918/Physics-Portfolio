Physics 1 — Motion & Displacement

Kinematics

Kinematics is the branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of objects without considering the forces that cause that motion. It analyzes how objects move through space and time, using quantities such as position, displacement, velocity, acceleration, and time.

The governing principle is that motion can be completely described by position as a function of time — from which all other kinematic quantities are derived. For uniformly accelerated motion, this description collapses into a set of predictable kinematic equations.

Research Support

Kinematic equations were formalized during the Scientific Revolution, with Galileo Galilei's inclined plane experiments (c. 1604) demonstrating that objects in free fall undergo constant acceleration — a foundational insight that later informed Newton's laws. Modern applications span robotics, aerospace engineering, biomechanics, and sports science.

In autonomous systems design — a field directly relevant to the author's own research — kinematics underpins trajectory planning: the process of computing how a mechanism must move through space over time to accomplish a target task.

" Every weekday morning in Halwan, I commute on the school bus. One morning, I started timing the approximate duration of our acceleration from rest to cruising speed (roughly 60 km/h in about 8 seconds). Applying x = v₀t + ½at², I could estimate the distance covered during that phase. Physics had turned my daily commute into a moving kinematic problem.

Reflection

Kinematics was the first physics topic that made me feel like I was learning a language rather than memorizing formulas. Once you understand position, velocity, and acceleration as related quantities, you begin reading the motion of everything around you differently — a car braking, a ball thrown across a court, an elevator decelerating as it approaches a floor.

Physics 2 — Electric Fields & Energy Storage

Capacitance

Capacitance is the ability of a system to store electric charge per unit of electric potential difference. A capacitor is a two-conductor device that exploits this property: when a voltage is applied, equal and opposite charges accumulate on the two conductors, creating an electric field that stores energy.

The defining principle is that the charge stored is directly proportional to the voltage applied. A larger capacitance means more charge can be stored at the same voltage.

Research Support

Capacitors are among the most widely used passive components in electronics, with applications ranging from power supply filtering to signal coupling and energy storage. The principle was first described quantitatively by Michael Faraday in the 19th century.

Modern supercapacitors push the limits, achieving capacitances in the range of thousands of Farads by maximizing plate surface area at the nanoscale — increasingly used in renewable energy systems and regenerative braking in electric vehicles.

" While building the circuit for my Autonomous Bio-Inspired Shading System, I integrated a small capacitor into the power management subsystem to smooth out voltage fluctuations when the ESP32 drew sudden bursts of current. Before adding it, the system would occasionally reboot. Once it was in place, the voltage supply stabilized. A physics concept I had studied on paper had just saved my research project.

Reflection

Capacitance was the moment physics stopped being abstract and became engineering. When I read C = Q/V for the first time, it seemed like a dry ratio. But when that same formula explained why my circuit stabilized after adding a 100μF capacitor, it became something I trusted and used.

Physics 2 — Current, Voltage & Resistance

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law states that the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it and inversely proportional to its resistance, provided temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.

Formulated by Georg Simon Ohm in 1827, this linear empirical relationship (V = IR) is the foundation of circuit analysis, enabling engineers to predict and control current flow in virtually any electronic system.

Research Support

Ohm's Law is the single most universally applied principle in electrical engineering. In microcontroller-based systems such as the Arduino or ESP32, it is essential for calculating correct resistor values for GPIO protection, LED brightness control, and sensor interfacing.

A critical safety application is in fuse and circuit breaker design: the power dissipation formula P = I²R quantifies how much heat a resistive element generates — the basis of both heating appliances and thermal safety engineering.

" During the development of the Micro Self-Irrigation System, I needed to drive a small water pump using the ESP32's GPIO pin. I used R = V/I to find the correct base resistor value for the transistor switch. Without Ohm's Law, I would have been guessing — and a wrong guess could have permanently damaged the microcontroller. Physics had just protected my hardware.

Reflection

Ohm's Law is deceptively simple — three variables, one equation. But its implications span every circuit I have ever touched. What struck me most was how this law, derived empirically by Georg Ohm in the early 19th century with no knowledge of electrons or quantum mechanics, holds with remarkable accuracy in virtually every circuit I work with today.

Final Thoughts

How Physics Changed the Way I See the World

Before physics, I interacted with the world transactionally — things worked, and I used them. After two years of General Physics, I interact with it analytically. I do not just ride a bus anymore; I notice its acceleration profile. I do not just plug in a circuit board; I calculate whether the GPIO can handle the load before powering it on.

The three concepts in this portfolio represent three different scales of that shift. Kinematics taught me to read motion. Capacitance taught me to see hidden energy storage in components I handle daily. Ohm's Law gave me the clearest and most immediately practical tool in my engineering toolkit.

What unifies all three is the experience of recognition: that moment where something you encounter in daily life suddenly has a name, a formula, and a reason. That recognition does not stop. Once you have it, you cannot turn it off — and I mean that as a compliment to physics.

"The best models are the ones that remain predictive across contexts. Kinematics, capacitance, and Ohm's Law have all earned that trust — not just in textbooks, but in commutes, circuits, and extension cords."
